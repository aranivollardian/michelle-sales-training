Session 1

The BBQ Challenge: Positioning Yourself

Before you can sell anything, you need to know what you are selling and who you are selling it to. This session is about finding the words that make people lean in and ask you more.

The BBQ Challenge

Imagine you are at a backyard barbecue on a Saturday afternoon. Someone asks you "So, what do you do for work?" Most technical people answer this question badly. They either go too deep into the technology, or they are too vague. Neither gets the other person interested.

This exercise is the foundation of everything else in this programme. If you cannot explain what you do to a stranger at a BBQ in a way that makes them want to know more, you will struggle to do it on a sales call, in a proposal, or on LinkedIn.

The Problem

Most AI professionals describe what they build rather than what they solve. Buyers do not care about your tech stack. They care about their problem and whether you can fix it.

The Formula

1
Who you help

Name the type of person or business. Be specific. "Companies" is too broad. "Mid-size businesses with sales teams" is better.

2
The problem you solve

Describe the pain in plain language. What is the frustrating, time-consuming, or expensive thing they are dealing with?

3
How you solve it (simply)

One sentence on your approach. Use words like "automate", "build", "set up" rather than "deploy a multi-agent RAG pipeline".

4
The outcome

What changes for them? Saved time, reduced cost, better decisions, fewer errors. Make it tangible.

Example

"I help businesses automate their most tedious manual work using AI. So if a company has people spending hours every week copying data between spreadsheets, or manually researching leads online, I build tools that do it in minutes. One client saved about 20 hours a week on lead research alone."

Notice: no jargon, no mention of specific models or APIs, clear outcome, and it naturally invites a follow-up question.

Positioning Your AI Services

Positioning is the strategic decision about who you serve and what you are known for. Without clear positioning, you end up competing on price with every other freelancer who "does AI stuff".

The three questions

  1. Who is your ideal client? Industry, company size, role of the buyer. The more specific, the easier it is to find them.
  2. What is the one problem you solve better than anyone? Pick the thing where you have the strongest proof.
  3. Why should they choose you? What is different about working with you versus hiring an agency or using an off-the-shelf tool?
Key Principle

Position yourself as a specialist, not a generalist. "I build custom AI automation for mid-market e-commerce brands" will always beat "I do AI consulting for businesses". Specialists command higher fees, get more referrals, and close faster.

Pricing: value, not time

One of the biggest mistakes technical freelancers make is pricing by the hour. When you price by the hour, your client is buying your time. When you price by the project or by the outcome, your client is buying a result.

Start by understanding what the problem costs your client. If manual lead research costs them $80,000 a year in wages and you can automate 80% of it, your solution is worth $50,000+ to them, regardless of how long it took you to build.

Where to use your BBQ pitch

Once you have your pitch nailed, use it everywhere: your LinkedIn headline and summary, the opening line of cold outreach emails, your website hero section, the first 30 seconds of any sales call, and the way you introduce yourself in meetings.

Exercise

Write your BBQ pitch

Write 3 different versions. Each no longer than 3 sentences. Test them on someone who is not technical. If they ask a follow-up question, it is working.

Bonus: Try one version that does not mention "AI" at all. Focus purely on the outcome.

Session 2

Pipeline Building, Outreach, and Winning Conversations

Now that you can position yourself, it is time to start conversations. This session covers prospecting, running calls that convert, and handling objections with confidence.

Sales Mindset: The Basics

Calling to sell something can feel intimidating, especially if you have never done it. These principles will help you build confidence one conversation at a time.

"Hi, how are you?"

Something as simple as asking "hi, how are you?" and then actually pausing to hear the answer is powerful. Practise it with everyone: your barista, receptionists, colleagues. The pause is what makes it genuine.

Ask for help

People are wired to want to help. Opening with "I was wondering if you could help me?" shifts the dynamic immediately. You are not pushing a product. You are a human being asking for assistance.

Example Opening

"Hi Susan, it's Michelle. I was wondering if you could help me please?"

They do not know who you are, but because you are asking for help, they will most likely say "yeah, sure." Now you have permission to talk.

Non-verbal communication (even on the phone)

Even on a call, the other person can hear your energy. Smile (you can hear a smile), use your hands while talking, and stand up and walk around if you can.

The 30 to 60 second challenge

Try to talk to the other person for 30 to 60 seconds without saying who you are or why you are calling. Chat about the weather, ask about their day. Build rapport before business.

Give 2 options

Rather than "what works for you?", give two specific options: "Does Thursday at 2pm or Monday at 3pm work?" Clear choices force a decision.

Ask for 15 minutes

Nobody is too busy to help someone for just 15 minutes. Once you are in the meeting, the conversation will naturally extend if there is genuine interest.

The Elevator Pitch

Your elevator pitch needs to sound like you are explaining your work to a mate at a BBQ. Write it up and keep it visible until you have it memorised.

Key Principle

If your pitch sounds like a pitch, it is not good enough yet. Rewrite it until it feels like something you would actually say to a friend.

Cold Calling Framework

Calling is 10% what you say and 90% how you say it. An opening that acknowledges you are interrupting their day shows honesty and empathy.

The opening

Try: "I didn't catch you at a bad time, did I?" People instinctively want to say "no", which gives you permission to continue.

Subtle commands

The word "listen" is a gentle command. Using the prospect's name makes them more attentive: "Listen, the reason I'm calling, Sarah, is that most businesses are finding it incredibly challenging to..."

Waterfall your call

Think of your call as water flowing down a mountain. Plan for key questions that act as "rocks" to direct the conversation. Prepare for decision points, not every sentence.

Exercise

Map your waterfall

Map out a call flow for your service. Start with your intro, identify the 2 to 3 likely responses, and prepare follow-ups for each until you reach a booked meeting or clear next step.

Objection Handling

When someone objects, it is the beginning of a real conversation, not the end.

1
Qualify the objection

Ask questions. Listen carefully. Do not interrupt.

2
Ask "what else?"

Keep asking until they have nothing more. Now you have the complete list.

3
Respond to the objection itself

Address the specific concern. If your solution is not right, be honest.

4
Document it

Write down how you handled it. Build a library of responses over time.

The Rapid Pitch Development Method

This technique fast-tracks your sales skills from weeks down to days.

1
Let go of outcomes

The goal is to build your pitch muscle, not close a deal.

2
Pitch One

Pick one style. Call 10 to 20 people back to back. Record what worked.

3
Pitch Two

Switch to a completely different style. Another 10 to 20 calls. Record data.

4
Repeat with 4 to 5 styles

Do this across a full day.

5
Build your own

Like LEGO, take the best pieces from each and build something uniquely yours.

Getting a Meeting via a Call

1
Use their name immediately

"Hi Wendy, lovely to meet you."

2
Simple, clear introduction

One sentence. No jargon.

3
Communicate the value

Why should they give you 15 minutes?

4
Give choices

"Does Thursday afternoon or Friday morning work better?"

5
Ask, then stop talking

Let the silence work for you.

Be Honest, Be Present

Find the top 3 things you love about what you do and lead with those. If you do not know the answer, say so and follow up later. Listening is the most important skill in sales. If what you are offering is the right solution, the conversation will flow naturally.

Session 3

Data Management in Sales: Setting Up Your CRM

A CRM is where you track every interaction with prospects and clients. Without a system, you will lose deals. It is that simple.

Why This Matters for You

As someone from a technical background, you intuitively understand structured data. A CRM is just a database for your sales activity. The discipline of logging everything is what separates people who "do some freelance work" from people who run a real practice.

Setting up HubSpot (Free Tier)

We recommend HubSpot's free CRM to start. It is powerful enough for a solo practitioner and scales well.

1
Create your account

Go to hubspot.com and sign up for the free CRM. Use your work email.

2
Connect your email

Settings >> Integrations >> Email Integrations. Connect Gmail. Tick "Log" to have emails logged against deals. Tick "Track" to see when people open your emails.

3
Add your contacts

With Gmail connected, contacts are created automatically. Otherwise: Contacts >> Create Contact. Work emails (e.g. john@acme.com) auto-create a Company record.

4
Set up your Deals pipeline

Each Deal = a potential project. View as Board (Kanban) or Table. Always associate the Contact and Company to each Deal so emails sync.

5
Log everything

Inside each Deal: log calls, meetings, emails. Emails sync automatically. Calls and meetings: log manually using the buttons inside the Deal.

Standard Notes Format

Every call or meeting note should follow this structure:

FieldWhat to capture
AttendeesWho attended and who missed
PrioritiesTop 3 priorities of the person you met with
PitchedTop 3 things you pitched or discussed
Next StepsSpecific actions with dates and owners
Follow Up TaskCreate a task in HubSpot with a due date
The Golden Rule

If it is not in the CRM, it did not happen. Log activity immediately after every interaction. The 2 minutes it takes will save you 20 minutes trying to remember what you discussed a week later.
